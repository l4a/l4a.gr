Searching for a non-existent Holy Grail

Every human activity has two dimensions to it, one technical and one aesthetic. Lighting is no exception. Applications that deal more with the technical dimension (such as road lighting) can easily be described as good or bad and can be easily documented by norms and guidelines. When it comes to applications that are more aesthetically-oriented (such as facade lighting), it’s really hard to tell.

So is there a Holy Grail of good lighting? Given that there is no common sense definition of good in this context, the answer has to be NO. There are no 100% sure-fire rules and guidelines for good lighting especially when the aesthetic dimension is at the fore. Here are just some of my personal criteria in this subjective evaluation.

  • Lighting a 3D world
  • Contrast: It’s all in the luminance distribution
  • The enemy: human vanity

Lighting a 3D world

Let’s face it, we live in a 3D world, or to be more precise humans can only perceive 3 of the universe’s dimensions (in light of the latest theories, I’m not sure how many dimensions there are now).

I often see buildings with flat lighting that turns the 3D structure into a 2D surface. By flat I mean lack of depth, lack of shadows, and lack of scale. It’s true that lighting is sometimes deliberately designed to be flat for architectural reasons. It’s also true that flatness can have visual interest. However, in most cases, the image of a flat-lit building leaves no lasting impression.

In this case the lighting designer transformed a flat-lit surface into an eye-catching volume by simply adding an inner blue light that complemented the facade’s yellowish colour.

Contrast: It’s all in the luminance distribution

“When you are courting a nice girl an hour seems like a second. When you sit on a red-hot cinder a second seems like an hour. That’s relativity.”

That’s how Einstein humorously described relativity. Bearing in mind that the human eye can adjust to a huge range of luminance (from 1E–6 cd/m2 to 300,000 cd/m2) one can easily understand that relativity also has to do with lighting. It doesn’t matter how much an object is lit, what matters is how much it is lit in relation to another object or its background and surroundings.

Absolute values of illuminance and luminance are useful as a starting point, but what’s more useful is knowledge of the materials of a facade and the luminance of the local site, for example. These are things that can define what we call contrast or luminance distribution. In the Greek countryside one can see a lot of heavily floodlit castles and monuments against a pitch black landscape. Looking at one of these from a distance you get the impression that the USS Enterprise has landed on the top of a hill. Lower lighting levels or graduated surrounding lighting could have helped a lot in cases like these.

Here’s the same stop frame with Grace Kelly and James Stewart (from Hitchcock’s Rear Window), in low contrast on the left and in high contrast on the right. Contrast triggers feelings. Master contrast and you master lighting. I think that’s a good rule of the thumb to follow.

The enemy: human vanity

Vanity is the reason for every disaster in human history. KISS stands for Keep It Simple and Small and this philosophy of minimalism and simplicity is free of human vanity (at least to a very large degree). Instead of taking a theoretical approach to how KISS can be applied to lighting, why not take a look at the pictures below.

This hotel features a simple lighting solution (a small floodlight in every interior corner) and follows the architectural pattern. Repeating lighting gradients creates a very interesting composition (despite the poor maintenance and the poor quality photo taken from a phone camera).

On the other hand this hotel’s lighting (located on the same road 2–3 Km away) looks pretentious and cheap (the blue light makes it even more pretentious and cheap looking).

You can either choose to attract a passer-by’s gaze by lighting a building properly or by lighting it badly. Bad lighting may help get more people looking at the building in the short-term, but for commercial establishments sooner or later it will result in a loss of profits. The majority of people may not be able to clearly articulate why the lighting is good or bad, but can certainly separate the good from the bad.

National road 1 that connects Athens with Salonica lies trough an industrial area near Athens. At about 60-70 Km from Athens you can see this excellent example of industrial lighting in which you can notice the right quantity of light, the right luminance distribution, the right choice of colors and the right modeling.

About 50km from this spot, just 10 or 20 Km from Athens you can see what is perhaps the worst lit building on the entire planet (I tried not to over or under expose the photo). The lighting here does something I’m not even sure David Copperfield could do: it makes the building disappear!

Approaching the building at night, you can see sky glow from kilometres away with this intolerable floodlighting. Just some of the issues it raises are light trespass, light pollution and the undoubted annoyance to local animals. However, these are minor compared to the risk of car accidents because of the extreme glare. Drivers find themselves confronted with thousands of candelas.

The funny thing is that the publishing company that owns this paper distribution plant has hosted many articles about light pollution in some of the dozens of newspapers, magazines and websites it owns.
