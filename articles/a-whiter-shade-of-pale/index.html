A whiter shade of pale

Filed under: Theory, Viewpoints

About 70 years have past since Kruithof published his famous curve that in raw words claimed that “the lower the lighting levels - the lower the color temperature”.

Recently i read a lot of articles regarding the benefits of “white light” in the city nightscape compared to the “yellow light”. Articles that more or less put into question Kruithof’s curve usage. Of course Kruithof’s work is not a gospel and we also have to define the terms “white” and “yellow”, but let’s put the word “why” in front of the claim “the lower the lighting levels - the lower the color temperature”.

For centuries and centuries humanity had only two sources to enlight its deep dark nights: the moon and the fire. Both of them have a characteristic yellowish shade and provide low light levels. During the day the white sunlight and the bluish skylight provide extremely hight light levels. I think this can interpret Kruithof’s research results. There’s no doubt that since early 40s nights became brighter and brighter. Proposals that re-evaluate and modify Kruithof’s curve have been published. Although the link between man and these primary lighting conditions is so strong that can’t easily break.

People often saying “white light” mean light with good color rendition properties, having in mind probably the old low pressure sodium (LPS) monochromatic lamps that made everything look yellow (they are still in use in Greece). Nowdays the new high pressure sodium lamps (HPS), the metal halide lamps and the LEDs with low CCT can provide the necessary warm tone together with excellent color rendition. I think that regarding urban lighting 3000 K must be color temperature’s upper limit.

In monuments and facades can be used colder light tones but when we talk about streets, parks, squares, etc. the good old Kruithof’s curve must be taken in consideration avoiding solutions (e.g. mercury vapour, metal halide & LEDs with high CCT) that make places look unfriendly.
