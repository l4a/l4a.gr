A whiter shade of pale

Home › Articles ›

It’s almost 70 years since the famous Kruithof curve was published. It made the bold claim that “the lower the lighting levels, the lower the colour temperature.”

Recently I’ve been reading a lot of articles regarding the benefits of white light in the city nightscape compared to yellow light. Those articles more or less call into question the Kruithof curve. Of course, Kruithof’s work isn’t gospel. When talking about light, it’s also essential to define the terms white and yellow. It’s also essential to ask ourselves why is it that the lower the lighting levels, the lower the colour temperature.

For centuries humanity only had two sources to enlighten the long, dark nights: the moon and fire. Both of them have a characteristic yellowish shade and provide low light levels. During the day the white sunlight and bluish light of the sky provide extremely high light levels. I think this may help explain Kruithof’s research results. There’s no doubt that since the early 1940s nights have become increasingly brighter. In light of this, proposals that re-evaluate and modify Kruithof’s curve have been published. However, the link between man and these primary lighting conditions is so strong that it can’t easily be broken.

People often say white light means light with good colour rendition properties. What they probably have in mind are old low-pressure sodium (LPS) monochromatic lamps that made everything look yellow (and of course, these are still in use in Greece). Nowadays, new high-pressure sodium lamps (HPS), metal halide lamps and LEDs with low CCT can provide the necessary warm tone together with excellent colour rendition. With urban lighting, I believe 3,000 K has to be the colour temperature’s upper limit.

Colder light tones can be used on monuments and facades. When talking about streets, parks and squares, the good old Kruithof curve must be taken in consideration, helping us avoid solutions (such as mercury vapour, metal halide & LED lights with high CCT) that make places look cold and unwelcoming.
